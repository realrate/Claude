## Mon 5 May — Back to building: when to hand it off

There's a moment in prototyping where you stop learning and start polishing.

I've had to get better at recognising that moment.

When I prototype something with an AI agent, it moves fast. An idea in the morning, something working by the afternoon. That speed is addictive.

But the question isn't whether it works. It's whether it works well enough to hand over, or well enough to ship.

Those are different standards. Confusing them wastes everyone's time.

My rule now: if the prototype proves the idea, hand it off. Don't improve it. The development team will rebuild it properly anyway. The point of the prototype was the question, not the answer.

What I'm still figuring out is how early to involve them. Too early and the idea isn't proven. Too late and they're cleaning up something that was never meant to scale.

I don't have a clean answer. But the mistake most founders make is holding on too long — treating the prototype as the product instead of evidence for the product.

## Thu 8 May — Back to building: what speed reveals

Fast prototyping changes your product decisions in ways you don't anticipate.

When a new feature takes a quarter to build, you argue about it first. Meetings, debates, written specs. The decision feels weighty because the cost of being wrong is high.

When it takes a morning, you just try it.

What I've found: a lot of things I would have argued for in a meeting don't survive contact with reality. And some things I would have talked myself out of actually work.

The filter changes. You're no longer selecting ideas based on how confidently you can defend them. You're selecting based on what holds up when you build it.

I think that's a better filter.

It also means I've stopped having certain conversations. The ones where you spend an hour discussing whether something is worth building before anyone has tried. Now I'd rather spend that hour trying.

## Mon 12 May — Finance AI: what explainable actually means

Most people hear "explainable AI" and assume it means a chart. A number. A score with a colour.

That's not what it means.

When an AI evaluates a company's financial health, the explanation is the reason. Not the result — the reason. What drove it. Which variables moved it. How sensitive the outcome is to each one.

Without that, you have a verdict without a case.

I've been building this since 2016. The hardest part was never the model. It was deciding what counts as an explanation for someone making a real decision.

A CFO approving credit. A fund manager evaluating a position. An analyst writing a report. They don't need a probability. They need to understand what they're looking at and why it matters.

That's a different design problem than making a model accurate. And it's the one most AI companies don't try to solve.

## Thu 15 May — Finance AI: causal vs pattern-matching

Most AI finds patterns. That's not the same as finding causes.

A pattern: companies with high revenue tend to have higher valuations.

A cause: equity cushion, liability structure, and cash-flow volatility drive financial stability — and you can measure the contribution of each one.

The distinction matters more than it sounds.

Pattern-based AI tells you what usually happens. Causal AI tells you why this specific company is where it is.

For financial decisions, "usually" is not enough. You need to know whether the strength you're looking at is structural or coincidental. Whether it will hold under stress or unwind in the first difficult quarter.

That's what causal models are built to answer. Not what's correlated. What's actually driving the outcome.

We built RealRate on that distinction. It's why the results look different from what you'd get ranking companies by revenue or profit margin.

## Mon 19 May — Finance AI: 10 years, what changed

I started RealRate ten years ago with a clear assumption: financial AI would be adopted quickly by large institutions.

I was wrong about the speed.

The technology moved fast. The institutions didn't. Sales cycles that I expected to take months took years. Buyers who understood the product immediately still needed eighteen months to approve a vendor relationship.

What I underestimated was the weight of existing process. Large financial institutions have compliance requirements, vendor assessment procedures, security reviews. The quality of the product matters less than the cost of change.

What surprised me on the other side: the people inside those institutions who actually wanted this to exist. Risk officers, analysts, credit managers who had been doing the same manual process for fifteen years and knew it wasn't working. They weren't the blockers. They were the internal advocates.

Ten years in, I'm more patient about sales cycles and more focused on finding those advocates early.

The technology was always the easier part.

## Thu 22 May — Leadership: what you hire for now

A question I've been sitting with: if everyone can use coding agents, what are you actually hiring for?

The obvious answer is judgment. The ability to evaluate what the agent produces, spot the mistakes, know when to push further and when to hand it off.

But I think it goes deeper.

What I've noticed is that the people who use AI tools well are the ones who know what they're trying to build and why. The tool is fast. The direction still has to come from somewhere.

So when I think about hiring now, I care less about whether someone can write code and more about whether they can think clearly about a problem before touching any tool.

That's not new. But AI makes it more visible, faster. Someone without clear thinking produces confident-sounding nonsense at speed. Someone with it produces something useful in a morning.

The hiring filter hasn't changed. The signal is just louder now.

## Mon 26 May — Leadership: the founder divide

There have always been two types of founders.

The ones who build. And the ones who sell.

Most founders do both, but usually one comes naturally and the other is a discipline. I spent the first year of RealRate building. Then the company grew and I stopped entirely. Talking to customers and investors became the priority.

That divide — between the founder who builds and the one who sells — has always shaped how companies get made. Technical founders over-build and under-sell. Non-technical founders sell things that take longer to build than promised.

AI is making that divide less rigid.

I'm back to building things, not because I hired a developer, but because I don't need one for the first version. And I'm still talking to customers because that part doesn't get automated.

I don't know yet what that means for company building at scale. But I suspect the founders who figure out how to do both at once will have an advantage that wasn't available ten years ago.
